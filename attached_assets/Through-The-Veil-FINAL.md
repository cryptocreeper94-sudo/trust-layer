# CHAPTER 1: THE MARKED OUTSIDER — A LIFE IN DETAIL

## Thirty Years in the Fog

I have always been different. Disconnected from cultural norms. Repelled by what others gravitate toward. Not by deliberate choice—by instinct.

My life has been chaos. For thirty years, I drank. Not casually. Not socially. I drank like a man running from something he could not name. I drank like someone attempting to drown out a frequency that refused to stop humming in the back of his skull. For most of those years, I remained ignorant of what I was running from.

I was a baseball player. Not naturally gifted at first, but I made myself capable through relentless effort. I worked at it. I earned my position. Then I attended college on a scholarship, and I began drinking, and I destroyed everything I had built. The collapse happened that quickly. One decision—or perhaps a thousand small ones—and the structure I had erected crumbled to dust.

My understanding of life during that period reduced itself to a single imperative: drink, drink, drink. Women were drawn to me because they found me attractive, and I left a trail of broken relationships in my wake. I fathered children without planning. Chaos followed me everywhere. Chaos became my constant companion.

Yet through all of it, the question persisted: Why am I still here?

I have always considered my lack of self-confidence to be a fundamental flaw in my character. True self-confidence has eluded me entirely. This persists despite the fact that others observe me and declare me a fine physical specimen—a man in good health and decent shape. Yet I feel lesser than everyone around me. That remains the irony no one perceives.

What I know with certainty is this: if you were to ask ninety percent of the people who know me, they would describe me as the funniest, happiest, most carefree, friendliest, most helpful person they have ever encountered. When people look into my eyes, they perceive something that makes them uncomfortable. Not because I appear threatening. Not because I project anger. They become uncomfortable because they feel seen. Most people do not wish to be truly seen. They prefer to remain undisturbed within their illusions.

I have survived experiences that should have killed me. Thirty years of heavy drinking. Automobile accidents. Physical altercations. Falls from dangerous heights. Situations where any ordinary person would have been hospitalized or buried. Each time, I walked away unscathed.

I perceive myself as something like an avatar—a spirit housed temporarily within a body. I have come to believe that our physical forms constitute biological suits designed to contain the spirit that was breathed into us by the Father. We clearly exist here to learn something. To accomplish something.
